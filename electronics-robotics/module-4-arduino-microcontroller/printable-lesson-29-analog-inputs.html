📚 Key Words — Learn These!
🎤 ~
Analog Signal
A signal that can be any value in between, not just ON or OFF. Like a volume knob — it has a thousand levels!
🔋 01
Digital Signal
A signal that is only HIGH (1) or LOW (0) — nothing in between. Like a regular light switch: fully on or fully off.
🔄 A0
analogRead()
The Arduino command that reads an analog pin. Returns a number from 0 to 1023 representing 0V to 5V.
🔃 🗡
Potentiometer
A variable resistor with a twisty knob and 3 legs. The middle leg gives any voltage between 0V and 5V as you turn it.
⚡ Digital vs Analog — Two Different Worlds!
▮ Digital Signal
Voltage | ___ ___ ___ | | | | | | |___| |___| Time | Only HIGH or LOW!
Only 2 values: HIGH (5V) or LOW (0V). Like a light switch — fully on or fully off.
Used with: digitalRead(), digitalWrite().
Arduino pins 0–13 are digital.
■ Just 2 levels
~ Analog Signal
Voltage | * | * * | * * | * * Time | 1024 in-between levels!
1024 values: anywhere from 0 to 1023. Like a dimmer switch — every tiny step in between!
Used with: analogRead().
Arduino pins A0–A5 are analog.
~ 1024 levels
🔬 The Potentiometer — Your Analog Knob!
Arduino Uno Breadboard +-----------+ | 5V (red)|---[wire]------> Left Leg (power) | | | A0 (yel) |---[wire]------> Middle Leg (wiper) -- gives 0V to 5V | | | GND (blk)|---[wire]------> Right Leg (ground) +-----------+ Potentiometer (top view): +------------------+ | ( knob here ) | | twist me! | +----+------+------+ | | | Left Middle Right 5V A0 GND (power) (signal) (ground) Knob all left = 0V (reads ~0) Knob halfway = 2.5V (reads ~512) Knob all right = 5V (reads ~1023)
The middle leg is the magic one — it slides between 0V and 5V as you twist the knob!
💻 The Code — Reading Analog Values
Arduino sketch — analogRead()
// Lesson 29: Read a potentiometer on pin A0 void setup() { Serial.begin(9600); // open Serial Monitor } void loop() { int sensorValue = analogRead(A0); // 0 to 1023 float voltage = sensorValue * (5.0 / 1023.0); // convert to volts Serial.print("Raw Value: "); Serial.print(sensorValue); Serial.print(" | Voltage: "); Serial.println(voltage, 2); // 2 decimal places delay(200); // read 5 times per second }
Serial Monitor output (twist the knob!)
=== Analog Reader Ready! === Raw Value: 0 | Voltage: 0.00 V Raw Value: 256 | Voltage: 1.25 V Raw Value: 512 | Voltage: 2.50 V Raw Value: 768 | Voltage: 3.76 V Raw Value: 1023 | Voltage: 5.00 V
The Voltage Formula
voltage = reading × (5.0 ÷ 1023.0)
Each step = 0.00489 volts  |  1024 total steps
Quick Math:
512 × 0.00489 = 2.50 V
200 × 0.00489 = 0.98 V
800 × 0.00489 = 3.91 V

  • 1 Place the potentiometer on the breadboard — all 3 legs in different rows.
  • 2 Connect the left leg to Arduino 5V with a red wire.
  • 3 Connect the right leg to Arduino GND with a black wire.
  • 4 Connect the middle leg to Arduino A0 with a yellow wire.
  • 5 Upload the code, open Serial Monitor, and twist the knob slowly!
All three legs must be connected or the readings will jump around. The middle (wiper) leg is the signal — always connect it to an analog pin (A0–A5)!

🔬 4 Experiments You Did Today!
A
Watch the Numbers Change
Open Serial Monitor and twist the knob all the way left, then right.
Raw value goes from 0 all the way to 1023 — that is 1024 steps!
B
Voltage Math Check
Stop the knob at halfway (reading near 512). Calculate: 512 × (5 ÷ 1023).
You should get ~2.50V — the Arduino just did that math for you!
C
Wand vs Arduino Comparison
Set the Wand to DC Voltage. Put the red probe on the middle pot leg. Compare to the Serial Monitor.
Both instruments agree! Small differences (0.05V) are normal — no instrument is perfectly exact.
D
LED Speed Controller
Upload the bonus code. Connect an LED to pin 7. Twist the knob.
The LED blink speed changes with the knob! You built a real speed controller!
🏠 Try at Home: Light Sensor!
Swap the potentiometer for a photoresistor (LDR). Wire one leg to 5V through a 10kΩ resistor, and connect the middle point to A0 with GND on the other leg.

Now cover the sensor with your hand — watch the numbers in Serial Monitor drop! 🌞
"Bright light = high reading. Darkness = low reading. Sensors work just like a potentiometer!"
