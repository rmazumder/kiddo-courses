📚 Key Words — Learn These!
🚪 →|
Diode
A component that acts like a one-way door for electricity — current can only flow in ONE direction. Flip it backwards and everything stops!
💡 LED
LED
Stands for Light Emitting Diode. A special diode that releases energy as light instead of heat when current flows through it.
➕ +
Anode (+)
The positive entrance where electricity enters a diode or LED. On an LED it is the longer leg. Remember: Long = Plus!
➖ −
Cathode (−)
The negative exit where electricity leaves a diode or LED. On a diode it is marked by a stripe. On an LED it is the shorter leg.

🌈 Meet the LED — A Diode That Glows!
Long leg = Anode (+) Short leg = Cathode (-) Inside the LED: flat side = cathode (-)
Each LED color uses a different forward voltage — the amount of voltage it needs to turn on. Blue needs more energy than red, just like blue light has more energy than red light in physics!
Color Forward Voltage Fun Fact
Red ~1.8–2.0 V First LED color ever made (1962)!
Orange ~2.0–2.1 V Used in traffic lights
Yellow ~2.1–2.2 V Used in indicator lights
Green ~2.0–3.0 V Standard green = 2V, bright green = 3V
Blue ~3.0–3.4 V Invented in 1994 — won the Nobel Prize!
White ~3.0–3.4 V Actually a blue LED with yellow coating!

Each LED has its OWN resistor and its OWN path — this is called a parallel circuit! If one LED goes off, the others stay on.

🔬 4 Experiments You Did Today!
A
The One-Way Door
Connect a diode in a circuit, then flip it backwards.
LED goes off — the diode blocked backwards current like a one-way valve!
B
Rainbow Display Build
Wire all 6 LED colors in parallel, each with its own 330Ω resistor.
All 6 colors glow at once — a full rainbow from one 9V battery!
C
Reverse Polarity Test
Flip one LED in the rainbow display so it is backwards.
Only that LED goes off — the other 5 stay on because each has its own path!
D
Wand Check — Measure the Rainbow
Use your Multimeter (DC Volts, 20V range) across each lit LED.
Red reads ~1.9V, blue reads ~3.2V — each color has a different forward voltage!
